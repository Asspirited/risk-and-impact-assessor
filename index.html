ACC Matrix
Attributes, Components, Capabilities — a coverage heuristic from James Whittaker (Google) and 50 Quick Ideas to Improve Your Tests (Adzic & Evans). Map what your system does (Capabilities) through how it’s built (Components) against quality goals (Attributes).
  1. Name the system or feature you want to test
  2. List its Capabilities — what does it actually do? (user-facing behaviours)
  3. Break it into Components — how is it built? (modules, services, APIs, storage)
  4. Define Attributes — your quality goals (security, performance, reliability, accessibility…)
  5. Use the matrix intersections to find coverage gaps: which capability × component × attribute combinations have no test?
Source: James Whittaker & Jonathan Bach (Google); Gojko Adzic & David Evans, 50 Quick Ideas to Improve Your Tests (2014).

Socratic Questioning
Turn a vague risk or assumption into a precise, measurable statement. Enter what you think the risk is — the tool challenges it with probing questions, refines the statement, and tells you what it would take to measure it.
  1. Write your risk, assumption, or issue in plain language — don't worry about precision yet
  2. Read the refined statement — does it capture what you actually meant? If not, revise and re-run
  3. Work through the Socratic questions — each one exposes something you haven't pinned down
  4. Use the measurability guidance to define a scale, threshold, and metric (Gilb's Planguage: Scale, Meter, Must, Plan, Past)
  5. Take the refined, measurable statement into FMEA or the RAID analysis for quantified prioritisation
Sources: Socratic method (Plato, 5th century BC); Tom Gilb, Competitive Engineering (2005) — Planguage measurable requirements; Douglas Hubbard, How to Measure Anything (2014) — calibrated estimation; GQM: Basili & Weiss, NASA/SEL (1984).

Pre-mortem
Assume the project has already failed. Working backwards from that imagined failure forces people to voice concerns they'd normally suppress — and surfaces risks before they become real.
  1. Brief the team: "It is one year from now. The project has failed badly. We need to understand how."
  2. Everyone writes down the most plausible causes — independently, before discussing (silences groupthink)
  3. Collect the scenarios and look for patterns — causes that multiple people named independently are the highest-signal risks
  4. For each scenario, identify the earliest detectable warning sign — what would you see first?
  5. Turn the top scenarios into concrete preventive actions and add them to your risk register
Source: Gary Klein, Seeing What Others Don't (2013); Daniel Kahneman, Thinking, Fast and Slow (2011), Ch. 24. Widely used in product development, military planning, and medicine.

Bow-Tie Analysis
Maps the full risk picture around a single hazard event: threats and their preventive controls on the left, consequences and their recovery controls on the right. Makes gaps in your barrier strategy visible at a glance.
  1. Define the central hazard event — the specific thing that must not happen (not "things go wrong" — "the payment service is unavailable at go-live")
  2. Left side: identify all plausible threats (causes that could trigger the hazard) and what currently prevents each one
  3. Right side: identify all consequences if the hazard occurs and what controls would limit or recover from each
  4. Look for barriers marked as absent or weak — these are your highest-priority risk actions
  5. Use alongside FMEA (to score individual failure modes) and Pre-mortem (to stress-test your scenario thinking)
Source: Developed at Shell in the 1970s; formalised in IEC/ISO 31010 Risk Management standard. Widely used in aviation, oil & gas, healthcare, and nuclear industries.

FMEA
Failure Mode and Effects Analysis — identify potential failures, their effects, and prioritise by Risk Priority Number (Severity × Occurrence × Detection).
  1. Name the process or system under analysis
  2. For each step or component, ask: what could go wrong? — these are your failure modes
  3. For each failure mode, score three factors 1–10: Severity (how bad is the effect?), Occurrence (how likely is it?), Detection (how easily would you catch it?)
  4. RPN = S × O × D — higher scores demand more attention
  5. Focus first on high-RPN items and failures with Severity 9–10 regardless of RPN
Source: MIL-STD-1629A, US Department of Defense (1980); SAE J1739; widely adopted in manufacturing, aerospace, healthcare, and software.

5 Whys
Drill past symptoms to root cause by repeatedly asking why. Each answer becomes the subject of the next question — stop when you reach something you can actually fix.
  1. Write a clear, specific problem statement (not "things are slow" — "the nightly batch job takes 6 hours instead of 1")
  2. Ask: Why did this happen? — write down the most direct cause
  3. Take that cause and ask why again — you're going deeper, not sideways
  4. Repeat until you reach a root cause with a concrete countermeasure. This often takes 3–7 iterations, not exactly five
  5. If each "why" branches into multiple causes, trace the most impactful branch first
Source: Taiichi Ohno, Toyota Production System (1978). Widely used in Lean, Six Sigma, and post-incident reviews.

Kepner-Tregoe — Is / Is Not
Bounds a problem by systematically defining what it IS and what it IS NOT across four dimensions. The boundary between the two sides is where the cause lives.
  1. Write a precise problem statement — observable, factual, not an opinion
  2. For each dimension (What, Where, When, Extent): state what IS true and what you might expect but IS NOT true
  3. Ask: what is distinctive about the IS compared to the IS NOT? The distinction is the cause
  4. State the most likely cause — it must explain both what IS happening and why the IS NOT is unaffected
  5. Test the cause: if it were true, would it fully explain the IS/IS NOT pattern?
Source: Charles Kepner & Benjamin Tregoe, The Rational Manager (1965). Widely used in manufacturing, IT incident management, and aerospace.

Ishikawa
Fishbone (cause-and-effect) diagram — map contributing causes across six standard categories to understand what's driving a problem or outcome.
  1. State the effect clearly — the problem or outcome at the head of the fishbone
  2. Work across the six bones: People (skills, behaviour), Methods (processes, procedures), Machines (tools, technology), Materials (inputs, data), Measurement (how success is defined/tracked), Environment (context, constraints)
  3. Brainstorm causes under each category — don't filter at this stage
  4. Look for causes that appear under multiple categories — high likelihood these are genuine root contributors
  5. Use alongside 5 Whys to drill further into the most significant causes
Source: Kaoru Ishikawa, Guide to Quality Control (1968). Foundational in TQM, ISO 9001, and healthcare quality management.

GQM — Goal, Question, Metric
Every measurement must serve a goal. GQM starts from what you want to achieve, works out what questions would tell you if you're achieving it, then defines the metrics that answer those questions — so nothing is measured without a reason.
  1. Define the Goal: what object are you studying, with what purpose, focusing on which quality characteristic, from whose viewpoint?
  2. Generate Questions: what would you need to know to tell whether the goal is being achieved? Be specific — "Is coverage sufficient?" is not a question, "What percentage of payment paths have no automated test?" is
  3. Define Metrics: for each question, what observable data would answer it? Include how you'd collect it and what a baseline or threshold looks like
  4. Validate traceability: every metric must link to a question, every question to a goal — delete anything that doesn't trace back
  5. Revisit the goal after measurement — measurement changes understanding; goals sometimes need updating
Source: Basili & Weiss, NASA/Software Engineering Laboratory (1984); also Gojko Adzic & David Evans, 50 Quick Ideas to Improve Your Tests (2014).

PDCA
Plan–Do–Check–Act — a structured improvement cycle that treats every change as an experiment with a measurable outcome.
  1. Plan: define the problem, your hypothesis for improvement, and how you'll measure success — be specific
  2. Do: implement the change on a small, controlled scale — don't roll out globally yet
  3. Check: measure results against your prediction — did it work? By how much? Was the effect consistent?
  4. Act: if successful, standardise the change and widen the rollout; if not, use what you learned to refine the plan and repeat
  5. Each cycle should be short and scoped — the value is in repeated rapid iteration, not one large experiment
Source: Walter Shewhart (1939), popularised by W. Edwards Deming. Foundational in ISO standards, Lean, and continuous improvement frameworks.

Value Stream Map
Value Stream Mapping — trace the full flow of work from request to delivery, expose waste and delays, and design a leaner future state.
  1. Define your value stream — pick a specific product family or workflow from trigger to delivery
  2. Map the current state: every step, handoff, wait time, queue, and information flow as they actually are — not as intended
  3. Identify waste: delays (waiting), rework (defects), over-processing, unnecessary handoffs, inventory build-up between steps
  4. Calculate total lead time vs value-add time — the ratio is often surprising
  5. Design the future state map with waste removed, then build a time-boxed implementation plan to close the gap
Source: Mike Rother & John Shook, Learning to See (1998), Lean Enterprise Institute. Adapted from Toyota's material and information flow mapping.

DORA Metrics
Four metrics that reliably predict software delivery performance and organisational outcomes — from the six-year DORA research programme (Accelerate).
  1. Deployment Frequency: how often does your team deploy to production? (Elite: multiple times/day; Low: less than once/month)
  2. Lead Time for Changes: from commit to production, how long? (Elite: under one hour; Low: over six months)
  3. Change Failure Rate: what % of deployments cause a degraded service or require remediation? (Elite: 0–5%; Low: 46–60%)
  4. Time to Restore Service: when an incident occurs, how long to recover? (Elite: under one hour; Low: over six months)
  5. Your band (Elite / High / Medium / Low) reflects where you sit across all four metrics — use it to identify which metric is your constraint
Source: DORA Research Programme; Nicole Forsgren, Jez Humble & Gene Kim, Accelerate: The Science of Lean Software and DevOps (2018). Annual State of DevOps Report.
