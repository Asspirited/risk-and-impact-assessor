ACC Matrix
Attributes, Components, Capabilities — a coverage heuristic from James Whittaker (Google) and 50 Quick Ideas to Improve Your Tests (Adzic & Evans). Map what your system does (Capabilities) through how it’s built (Components) against quality goals (Attributes).

  4. Define Attributes — your quality goals (security, performance, reliability, accessibility…)

Socratic Questioning
Turn a vague risk or assumption into a precise, measurable statement. Enter what you think the risk is — the tool challenges it with probing questions, refines the statement, and tells you what it would take to measure it.
  1. Write your risk, assumption, or issue in plain language — don't worry about precision yet
  2. Read the refined statement — does it capture what you actually meant? If not, revise and re-run
  3. Work through the Socratic questions — each one exposes something you haven't pinned down
  4. Use the measurability guidance to define a scale, threshold, and metric (Gilb's Planguage: Scale, Meter, Must, Plan, Past)
  5. Take the refined, measurable statement into FMEA or the RAID analysis for quantified prioritisation
Sources: Socratic method (Plato, 5th century BC); Tom Gilb, Competitive Engineering (2005) — Planguage measurable requirements; Douglas Hubbard, How to Measure Anything (2014) — calibrated estimation; GQM: Basili & Weiss, NASA/SEL (1984).

SWOT Analysis
Maps the strategic risk landscape: internal Strengths and Weaknesses, external Opportunities and Threats. Used to expose the gap between where you are and where you need to be before committing to a plan.
  1. Strengths: internal advantages you control — skills, resources, capabilities, existing relationships
  2. Weaknesses: internal disadvantages — gaps in capability, resource constraints, technical debt, team limitations
  3. Opportunities: external conditions you could exploit — market changes, competitor gaps, new technology, regulatory shifts
  4. Threats: external conditions that could harm you — competitor moves, dependency risks, regulatory changes, economic factors
  5. After identifying all four quadrants, look for SO strategies (use strengths to exploit opportunities), ST strategies (use strengths to mitigate threats), WO strategies (use opportunities to overcome weaknesses), and WT strategies (minimise weakness exposure to threats)
Source: Learned, Christensen, Andrews & Guth, Harvard Business School (1960s). Popularised by Ansoff; widely used in strategy, product planning, and project risk.

Pre-mortem
Assume the project has already failed. Working backwards from that imagined failure forces people to voice concerns they'd normally suppress — and surfaces risks before they become real.
  1. Brief the team: "It is one year from now. The project has failed badly. We need to understand how."
  2. Everyone writes down the most plausible causes — independently, before discussing (silences groupthink)
  3. Collect the scenarios and look for patterns — causes that multiple people named independently are the highest-signal risks
  4. For each scenario, identify the earliest detectable warning sign — what would you see first?
  5. Turn the top scenarios into concrete preventive actions and add them to your risk register
Source: Gary Klein, Seeing What Others Don't (2013); Daniel Kahneman, Thinking, Fast and Slow (2011), Ch. 24. Widely used in product development, military planning, and medicine.

Bow-Tie Analysis
Maps the full risk picture around a single hazard event: threats and their preventive controls on the left, consequences and their recovery controls on the right. Makes gaps in your barrier strategy visible at a glance.
  1. Define the central hazard event — the specific thing that must not happen (not "things go wrong" — "the payment service is unavailable at go-live")
  2. Left side: identify all plausible threats (causes that could trigger the hazard) and what currently prevents each one
  3. Right side: identify all consequences if the hazard occurs and what controls would limit or recover from each
  4. Look for barriers marked as absent or weak — these are your highest-priority risk actions
  5. Use alongside FMEA (to score individual failure modes) and Pre-mortem (to stress-test your scenario thinking)
Source: Developed at Shell in the 1970s; formalised in IEC/ISO 31010 Risk Management standard. Widely used in aviation, oil & gas, healthcare, and nuclear industries.

FMEA
Failure Mode and Effects Analysis — identify potential failures, their effects, and prioritise by Risk Priority Number (Severity × Occurrence × Detection).
  1. Name the process or system under analysis
  2. For each step or component, ask: what could go wrong? — these are your failure modes
  3. For each failure mode, score three factors 1–10: Severity (how bad is the effect?), Occurrence (how likely is it?), Detection (how easily would you catch it?)
  4. RPN = S × O × D — higher scores demand more attention
  5. Focus first on high-RPN items and failures with Severity 9–10 regardless of RPN
Source: MIL-STD-1629A, US Department of Defense (1980); SAE J1739; widely adopted in manufacturing, aerospace, healthcare, and software.

5 Whys
Drill past symptoms to root cause by repeatedly asking why. Each answer becomes the subject of the next question — stop when you reach something you can actually fix.
  1. Write a clear, specific problem statement (not "things are slow" — "the nightly batch job takes 6 hours instead of 1")
  2. Ask: Why did this happen? — write down the most direct cause
  3. Take that cause and ask why again — you're going deeper, not sideways
  4. Repeat until you reach a root cause with a concrete countermeasure. This often takes 3–7 iterations, not exactly five
  5. If each "why" branches into multiple causes, trace the most impactful branch first
Source: Taiichi Ohno, Toyota Production System (1978). Widely used in Lean, Six Sigma, and post-incident reviews.

Fault Tree Analysis
Starts from the undesired top-level event and works downwards: what combinations of failures could cause this? AND gates (all required) and OR gates (any one sufficient) reveal which failure paths are most dangerous — the ones with the fewest, most likely causes.
  1. Define the top event precisely — the specific failure you want to prevent (e.g. "customer payment fails at checkout")
  2. Ask: what immediate causes could produce this? For each, is it AND (all must occur) or OR (any one is sufficient)?
  3. Continue downwards — each intermediate event becomes the top event for the next level of the tree
  4. Stop at basic events — hardware failures, human errors, software faults — where no further decomposition is useful
  5. The most dangerous paths are OR gates with high-probability basic events — any one of them is enough to cause the top event
Source: H.A. Watson, Bell Labs (1961); formalised in IEC 61025 Fault Tree Analysis standard. Widely used in aerospace, nuclear, automotive, and software safety.

Kepner-Tregoe — Is / Is Not
Bounds a problem by systematically defining what it IS and what it IS NOT across four dimensions. The boundary between the two sides is where the cause lives.
  1. Write a precise problem statement — observable, factual, not an opinion
  2. For each dimension (What, Where, When, Extent): state what IS true and what you might expect but IS NOT true
  3. Ask: what is distinctive about the IS compared to the IS NOT? The distinction is the cause
  4. State the most likely cause — it must explain both what IS happening and why the IS NOT is unaffected
  5. Test the cause: if it were true, would it fully explain the IS/IS NOT pattern?
Source: Charles Kepner & Benjamin Tregoe, The Rational Manager (1965). Widely used in manufacturing, IT incident management, and aerospace.

Ishikawa
Fishbone (cause-and-effect) diagram — map contributing causes across six standard categories to understand what's driving a problem or outcome.
  1. State the effect clearly — the problem or outcome at the head of the fishbone
  2. Work across the six bones: People (skills, behaviour), Methods (processes, procedures), Machines (tools, technology), Materials (inputs, data), Measurement (how success is defined/tracked), Environment (context, constraints)
  3. Brainstorm causes under each category — don't filter at this stage
  4. Look for causes that appear under multiple categories — high likelihood these are genuine root contributors
  5. Use alongside 5 Whys to drill further into the most significant causes
Source: Kaoru Ishikawa, Guide to Quality Control (1968). Foundational in TQM, ISO 9001, and healthcare quality management.

GQM — Goal, Question, Metric
Every measurement must serve a goal. GQM starts from what you want to achieve, works out what questions would tell you if you're achieving it, then defines the metrics that answer those questions — so nothing is measured without a reason.
  1. Define the Goal: what object are you studying, with what purpose, focusing on which quality characteristic, from whose viewpoint?
  2. Generate Questions: what would you need to know to tell whether the goal is being achieved? Be specific — "Is coverage sufficient?" is not a question, "What percentage of payment paths have no automated test?" is
  3. Define Metrics: for each question, what observable data would answer it? Include how you'd collect it and what a baseline or threshold looks like
  4. Validate traceability: every metric must link to a question, every question to a goal — delete anything that doesn't trace back
  5. Revisit the goal after measurement — measurement changes understanding; goals sometimes need updating
Source: Basili & Weiss, NASA/Software Engineering Laboratory (1984); also Gojko Adzic & David Evans, 50 Quick Ideas to Improve Your Tests (2014).

SPC — Statistical Process Control
Distinguishes natural process variation (common cause — ignore it) from signals that something has genuinely changed (special cause — investigate it). Reacting to noise makes things worse. SPC tells you which is which.
  1. Collect a series of measurements over time — defect counts, cycle times, error rates, test failure rates. At least 20–25 data points to establish stable limits
  2. Calculate the mean and control limits (typically ±3σ). Points outside the limits, or non-random patterns within them (runs of 8, trends), are special cause signals
  3. Special cause: stop and investigate — something has changed. Common cause: the process is stable — improve the process itself, not the individual point
  4. Describe your data series and what you're seeing — the tool will identify signal vs noise and advise whether to investigate or improve the system
Source: Walter Shewhart, Economic Control of Quality of Manufactured Product (1931); W. Edwards Deming, Out of the Crisis (1982). Applied to software by Donald Wheeler, Understanding Variation (1993).

Test Quadrants
Maps testing across two axes: business-facing vs technology-facing, and supporting the team vs critiquing the product. Reveals which quadrant is being neglected — the one you're missing is usually the source of your quality problems.
  1. Q1 (Tech-facing, support team): unit tests, component tests — automated, fast, developer-owned
  2. Q2 (Business-facing, support team): functional tests, story tests, examples — collaborative, specification by example
  3. Q3 (Business-facing, critique product): exploratory testing, usability testing, user acceptance — human judgement, not scripted
  4. Q4 (Tech-facing, critique product): performance, security, reliability — tools-assisted, specialist knowledge
  5. Describe your current testing approach — the tool identifies which quadrants are weak and what that means for your quality risks
Source: Brian Marick (2003, agile testing matrix blog); Lisa Crispin & Janet Gregory, Agile Testing (2009). Q4 additions by Gojko Adzic.

PDCA
Plan–Do–Check–Act — a structured improvement cycle that treats every change as an experiment with a measurable outcome.
  1. Plan: define the problem, your hypothesis for improvement, and how you'll measure success — be specific
  2. Do: implement the change on a small, controlled scale — don't roll out globally yet
  3. Check: measure results against your prediction — did it work? By how much? Was the effect consistent?
  4. Act: if successful, standardise the change and widen the rollout; if not, use what you learned to refine the plan and repeat
  5. Each cycle should be short and scoped — the value is in repeated rapid iteration, not one large experiment
Source: Walter Shewhart (1939), popularised by W. Edwards Deming. Foundational in ISO standards, Lean, and continuous improvement frameworks.

Value Stream Map
Value Stream Mapping — trace the full flow of work from request to delivery, expose waste and delays, and design a leaner future state.
  1. Define your value stream — pick a specific product family or workflow from trigger to delivery
  2. Map the current state: every step, handoff, wait time, queue, and information flow as they actually are — not as intended
  3. Identify waste: delays (waiting), rework (defects), over-processing, unnecessary handoffs, inventory build-up between steps
  4. Calculate total lead time vs value-add time — the ratio is often surprising
  5. Design the future state map with waste removed, then build a time-boxed implementation plan to close the gap
Source: Mike Rother & John Shook, Learning to See (1998), Lean Enterprise Institute. Adapted from Toyota's material and information flow mapping.

Flow Metrics
Cycle Time, Throughput, WIP, and Flow Efficiency — four measurements that let you predict delivery without story points or estimates. Based on Little's Law: WIP = Throughput × Cycle Time.
  1. Describe your current flow situation — how long items typically take, how many are in flight, how many you complete per week
  2. Cycle Time is elapsed time from start to done for a single item — measure at the 50th and 85th percentile, not the average
  3. Throughput is items completed per time period — count items, not points or hours
  4. WIP is everything started but not done — high WIP inflates cycle time (Little's Law: CT = WIP ÷ Throughput)
  5. Flow Efficiency = active work time ÷ total elapsed time. Most teams run at 15–20% — the rest is waiting
Source: J.D.C. Little, "A Proof for the Queuing Formula" (1961); Dan Vacanti, Actionable Agile Metrics for Predictability (2015); David Anderson, Kanban (2010).

DORA Metrics
Four metrics that reliably predict software delivery performance and organisational outcomes — from the six-year DORA research programme (Accelerate).
  1. Deployment Frequency: how often does your team deploy to production? (Elite: multiple times/day; Low: less than once/month)
  2. Lead Time for Changes: from commit to production, how long? (Elite: under one hour; Low: over six months)
  3. Change Failure Rate: what % of deployments cause a degraded service or require remediation? (Elite: 0–5%; Low: 46–60%)
  4. Time to Restore Service: when an incident occurs, how long to recover? (Elite: under one hour; Low: over six months)
  5. Your band (Elite / High / Medium / Low) reflects where you sit across all four metrics — use it to identify which metric is your constraint
Source: DORA Research Programme; Nicole Forsgren, Jez Humble & Gene Kim, Accelerate: The Science of Lean Software and DevOps (2018). Annual State of DevOps Report.

SPACE Framework
Developer productivity across five dimensions: Satisfaction, Performance, Activity, Communication/Collaboration, Efficiency. Corrects DORA's bias toward deployment metrics — productivity is multidimensional and can't be reduced to four numbers.
  1. Satisfaction: developer wellbeing, job satisfaction, burnout signals — measured by survey, eNPS, attrition
  2. Performance: outcomes of the work — reliability, quality, customer-facing metrics. Not output volume
  3. Activity: observable actions — PRs, code reviews, deployments. Important: activity ≠ productivity. Use as context, not as a productivity signal
  4. Communication/Collaboration: how work flows between people — review turnaround, knowledge sharing, onboarding time
  5. Efficiency & Flow: uninterrupted focus time, flow state, interruption rate, WIP per person
Source: Nicole Forsgren, Margaret-Anne Storey, Chandra Maddila, Thomas Zimmermann, Brian Houck, Jenna Butler — "The SPACE of Developer Productivity", ACM Queue (2021). Published by GitHub & Microsoft Research.

Cynefin Framework
Before choosing a method, understand what type of situation you're in. Cynefin has five domains — each requires a fundamentally different response. Using a complicated-domain tool on a complex problem makes things worse, not better.
  1. Clear: cause and effect are obvious. Best practice exists. Sense–Categorise–Respond. Don't over-complicate it
  2. Complicated: cause and effect require expert analysis but can be understood. Sense–Analyse–Respond. FMEA, RCA, and structured methods live here
  3. Complex: cause and effect only understood in retrospect. No best practice — run safe-to-fail experiments. Probe–Sense–Respond. Agile methods, A/B testing, hypothesis-driven delivery
  4. Chaotic: no discernible cause-effect relationship — act first to stabilise, then sense, then respond. Crisis management, incident response
  5. Disorder: you don't know which domain you're in. The most dangerous state — people default to their preferred domain regardless of fit
Source: Dave Snowden & Mary Boone, A Leader's Framework for Decision Making, Harvard Business Review (2007). Originally developed at IBM Global Services (late 1990s).

Theory of Constraints
Every system has exactly one constraint that limits its throughput. Improving anything that isn't the constraint doesn't improve the system — it only creates local optimisation and more inventory. Find the constraint, exploit it, subordinate everything else to it.
  1. Identify: what is the single step or resource limiting your throughput? Look for the longest queue, the most rework, the step everything waits behind
  2. Exploit: get maximum output from the constraint without spending money — remove waste from it, stop feeding it work it can't process, protect it with a buffer
  3. Subordinate: adjust everything else to support the constraint — stop optimising non-constraints; their idle time is fine if it keeps the constraint busy
  4. Elevate: if the constraint is still limiting after exploitation, invest to increase its capacity — hire, automate, redesign the step
  5. Repeat: once the constraint is broken, find the new one. The constraint moves — don't let inertia become the next constraint
Source: Eliyahu Goldratt, The Goal (1984); Critical Chain (1997). Applied to software by Clarke Ching, Gene Kim, and the DevOps movement.

7 Wastes (Lean)
Lean's seven categories of waste — originally from manufacturing, directly applicable to software delivery. Most teams are running at 15–20% value-adding activity. The other 80%+ is waste of one of these seven types.
  1. Transport: unnecessary handoffs, moving work between people or systems
  2. Inventory: unfinished work, backlogs, half-done features sitting in queues
  3. Motion: unnecessary tool switching, meeting interruptions, context switching
  4. Waiting: waiting for approval, review, environments, decisions, information
  5. Overproduction: building features no one uses, gold-plating, speculative work
  6. Over-processing: unnecessary steps, approvals, documentation, process overhead
  7. Defects: bugs, rework, fixing things that were done wrong — including in requirements
Source: Taiichi Ohno, Toyota Production System (1978); applied to software by Mary & Tom Poppendieck, Lean Software Development (2003).

DMAIC
Define–Measure–Analyse–Improve–Control. Six Sigma's structured problem-solving framework — ensures you understand the problem deeply before jumping to solutions, and confirms the improvement worked before closing it.
  1. Define: what is the problem, who is affected, what is the goal? Create a clear problem statement and project charter
  2. Measure: establish the current baseline — what data do you have? What is the current defect rate, cycle time, or failure frequency?
  3. Analyse: identify root causes — use 5 Whys, Ishikawa, or statistical analysis to find what is actually causing the problem
  4. Improve: design and test solutions — run pilots, measure impact against baseline, iterate
  5. Control: make the improvement stick — control plans, monitoring, handoff to operations so the problem doesn't return
Source: Motorola (Mikel Harry, 1980s); popularised by GE (Jack Welch, 1990s). Standardised by ASQ (American Society for Quality). Part of the Lean Six Sigma methodology.

WSJF — Cost of Delay
Ranks work by what you lose from delaying it, not just what it's worth. CD3 (Cost of Delay ÷ Duration) tells you which item costs the most per unit of time it sits waiting — that one goes first.
  1. For each piece of work, estimate its Cost of Delay: what value (in £/week, or a proxy score) is lost for every week it isn't done?
  2. Cost of Delay has three components: User/Business Value (what it's worth), Time Criticality (does the value decay or a deadline loom?), Risk Reduction or Opportunity Enabling (does it unblock something else?)
  3. Estimate Duration: how long will it take to deliver once started? Use relative sizes (1, 2, 3, 5, 8) if you can't give actual weeks
  4. CD3 = Cost of Delay ÷ Duration. Rank your items by CD3 score — highest CD3 goes first
  5. Black Swan Farming's insight: short items with high delay costs are almost always higher priority than long, valuable items — because the short item frees up the queue faster
Source: Don Reinertsen, The Principles of Product Development Flow (2009); Black Swan Farming, CD3 framework (Josh Arnold); SAFe WSJF model (Scaled Agile Framework).

Apollo RCA
Maps every causal path from a primary event back through its actions and conditions — not a single chain but a lateral web of causes. Each cause is verified or flagged as requiring evidence. Solutions are targeted at specific causes in the map, not the top-level symptom.
  1. State the primary event — the harm or problem you want to understand. Be precise: not "things went wrong" but "database went read-only at 14:07 UTC during peak load"
  2. Apollo distinguishes two types of cause: Actions (something someone or something did) and Conditions (a state that existed and enabled the action to cause harm)
  3. Problems have multiple causal paths, not one. Apollo maps branches — what else could have caused this or made it worse?
  4. For each cause, ask: what evidence do we have that this is true? If you can't verify it, it's a hypothesis — mark it as unverified and treat it as a gap to investigate
  5. Solutions that address causes deeper in the chain are more durable than those that address the event directly (which is treating the symptom)
Source: Dean Gano, Apollo Root Cause Analysis: A New Way of Thinking (4th ed., 2011). Widely used in healthcare (RCA²), aviation, nuclear, and industrial safety. Adopted by the Joint Commission (JCAHO) for sentinel event analysis.

OKR — Objectives & Key Results
Turn a vague goal into a structured commitment. The Objective sets inspiring direction. Each Key Result is a precise, measurable signal that tells you whether you got there — not an activity, not a milestone, but an outcome with a number attached.
  1. Describe your Objective in a single inspiring sentence — no numbers, no percentages. It should make people want to work on it
  2. Ask: "How will we know we've achieved this?" — each answer is a Key Result candidate
  3. Good Key Results are outcome-based (user retention up 20%) not output-based ("launch feature X"). If it's an activity, it's not a KR
  4. At the start of a cycle, your confidence in each KR should be around 50%. Too confident and it's sand-bagging; too low and it's fantasy
  5. Check in weekly or fortnightly — OKRs fail silently without regular scoring
Source: Andy Grove, High Output Management (1983) — originated OKRs at Intel. John Doerr, Measure What Matters (2018) — popularised at Google. Christina Wodtke, Radical Focus (2016).

Monte Carlo Simulation
Model schedule or cost uncertainty using 3-point estimates. Based on PERT (Program Evaluation and Review Technique) — each variable gets an optimistic, most-likely, and pessimistic estimate. The simulation aggregates them into P50, P80, and P90 confidence levels and surfaces the variables driving the most risk.
  1. List the major work items or cost components you want to model — anything with real uncertainty
  2. For each, provide three estimates in your chosen unit (days, weeks, £k, story points): Optimistic (best case, rare but possible), Most likely (your honest best guess), Pessimistic (bad-but-plausible, not catastrophic)
  3. The tool calculates a PERT-weighted mean for each variable: (O + 4M + P) / 6 — the most-likely case counts four times as heavily as the extremes
  4. P50 = most likely total outcome. P80 = outcome you'll beat 80% of the time. P90 = 90% confidence. The gap between P50 and P90 is your risk exposure
  5. Variables with the widest spread (high pessimistic vs optimistic ratio) are the key risk drivers — focus your mitigation there first
Source: PERT: US Navy / Booz Allen Hamilton (1957). Monte Carlo application to project management: Project Management Institute, PMBOK Guide; Douglas Hubbard, How to Measure Anything (2007); Mike Cohn, Agile Estimating and Planning (2005).
